Dr. Norman Borlaug

1914

Born in Cresco, Iowa

1933

Left his family's farm to attend the University of Minnesota, thanks to a Depression era program known as the "National Youth Administration"

1935

Stopped school and save up more money. Worked in the Civilian Conservation Corps, helping starving Americans. "I saw how food changed them", he said. "All of this left scars on me."

1937

Finished university and takes a job in the US Forestry Service

1938

Married wife of 69 years Margret Gibson. Got laid off due to budget cuts. Inspired by Elvin Charles Stakman, he returned to school study under Stakman, who taught him about breeding pest-resistent plants.

1941

Tried to enroll in the military after the Pearl Harbor attack, but was rejected. Instead, the military asked his lab to work on waterproof glue, DDT to control malaria, disinfectants, and other applied science.

1942

Received a Ph.D. in Genetics and Plant Pathology

1944

Rejected a 100% salary increase from Dupont, left behind his pregnant wife, and flew to Mexico to head a new plant pathology program. Over the next 16 years, his team bred 6,000 different strains of disease resistent wheat - including different varieties for each major climate on Earth.

1945

Discovered a way to grown wheat twice each season, doubling wheat yields

1953

Crossed a short, sturdy dwarf breed of wheat with a high-yeidling American breed, creating a strain that responds well to fertilizer. It went on to provide 95% of Mexico's wheat.

1962

Visited Delhi and brang his high-yielding strains of wheat to the Indian subcontinent in time to help mitigate mass starvation due to a rapidly expanding population

1970

Received the Nobel Peace Prize

1983

Helped seven African countries dramatically increase their maize and sorghum yields

1984

Became a distinguished professor at Texas A&M University

2005

Stated "we will have to double the world food supply by 2050." Argued that genetically modified crops are the only way we can meet the demand, as we run out of arable land. Said that GM crops are not inherently dangerous because "we've been genetically modifying plants and animals for a long time. Long before we called it science, people were selecting the best breeds."

2009

Died at the age of 95.
